深入黑部峽谷


平日早晨的宇奈月相當冷清，大部分店家都還沒開始營業，馬路上空蕩蕩的安靜異常。我覺得這裡有點像烏來，特別是被群山環繞的氛圍，原本想信步閒晃一下，但被郭大哥催促趕緊去買下一段車票。此刻距離發車時間還有半小時，我心想郭大哥未免太謹慎，沒料到一進車站，便驚覺大廳已經塞滿排隊的人潮。

有精通日語的 Ariel 和 Berny 押隊，買車票這種小事簡直像吃一塊小蛋糕，事實上這次預約山屋和諮詢登山道即時路況，也都是靠他們才能無痛完成。與我同樣不諳日語的領隊郭大哥擔負行程安排和資料查找的重責大任，全隊四人當中只有我毫無建樹。無腦跟團似乎已成為我近幾年的登山寫照。

疫情爆發以後，首先認識靖捷，當時他正為收集百岳而積極邀約，我只需將假期時間空下來並且坐等計畫書，偶爾突發奇想安插幾段自己想去的探索路線就心滿意足，實在不用多費什麼心。後來認識沛、怡華跟 Misha，他們總能找到好玩的行程，讓爬山不再只是點到點行軍式登頂山頭，而是用心體驗大自然一切事物。他們會在山上做瑜珈、冥想、畫畫，甚至只是躺著、坐著、聊著、安靜著，都讓人相信自己成為天地萬物的一分子。再後來認識郭大哥，他邀約的路線大多沒聽過，通常是地圖裡沒畫出來的或是次要的路線，不一定離家很遠，不一定很難走，也不一定需要很多天，但總是充滿故事性和神秘感。有時候就為了一堵殘破不堪的駁坎，或是隱藏在溪谷深處被草木遮掩的礦坑，難免腳踏泥濘，也難免披荊斬棘。雖然跟不同人爬山，風格大相徑庭，但無論跑得快或走得慢，我都能很放心把自己交給他們，可以一起經驗愉快的片刻，也可以合力克服路上遭遇的種種困境，於是我開始越來越依賴，越來越懶得從路線規劃開始經歷一系列繁瑣的前置工作。幸好大家對我都很包容，讓我可以毫無壓力地接受自己一次又一次在隊伍中當米蟲。


身為稱職米蟲，這次行前我甚至沒有查閱黑部峽谷鐵道的由來，直到回國許久才為了寫紀錄而補做功課。這條鐵道最早是在一戰後由日本電力出資興建，為了開發黑部川中游的水力資源，建設發電廠，以因應戰後工業化對電力的大量需求。二戰後日本電力解散，包含軌道在內的所有設施幾經讓渡後由関西電力繼承，最終從宇奈月到欅平間這段二戰前便已投入使用、稱為「下部軌道」的路段則被開發為載客的觀光路線，並在 2013 年和阿里山森林鐵路締結為姊妹鐵道，還推出票證互惠活動。

黑部峽谷鐵道乘車券有分座艙，我們買的是位於列車前半段的開放式車廂，雖說有天篷可以遮陽，實則車內只有板凳和欄杆，以及象徵意義大於實際意義的安全鍊條，很像遊樂園中的遊園車。這次因為是夏季來，天氣又涼爽乾燥，坐敞篷覺得很有趣，如果冬天或遇上下雨，大概會備受折磨吧！列車後半段是封閉式車廂，不怕風吹雨淋，但隔著一層窗戶看風景，似乎太有距離而讓有趣程度大幅下降了。


隨著尖銳喇叭聲劃破寂靜溫泉鄉，機車頭怒吼發出巨大且低沉的轟鳴，列車搖晃著緩緩駛過重重道岔，穿行粗糙而窄小的漆黑隧道。跨越黑部川高處殷紅色的新山彥鐵拱橋之時，沿岸眾人紛紛停下手邊工作，熱情向列車揮手致意，我們就這樣抱著既期待又不捨的心情告別宇奈月溫泉，深入黑部峽谷的旅程於焉展開。

雖說是觀光鐵道，但過去的產業痕跡仍然銘刻在舉目望去每一處，其實黑部峽谷鐵道從未卸下產業運輸的任務，依然是関西電力用以維繫黑部川諸多水壩和電廠運作的命脈。經過新山彥鐵橋來到對岸，很快就抵達位於宇奈月水壩上方的柳橋站，這裡並非觀光車站，而是供新柳河原發電所物資及人員出入的據點，因此列車只是慢速經過而並未停靠；除了新柳河原發電所以外，我們在抵達欅平前還會陸續經過出し平水壩、黑部川第二發電所和小屋平水壩，這些地方完全沒有其他聯外的交通方式，可見僅有的鐵道扮演多麼關鍵的角色。當然中途還是有兩座車站允許遊客上下車，第一站是位於黒薙川匯流口的黒薙站，在這邊下車這裡可以徒步前往黒薙溫泉、黒薙第二發電所、北又谷，甚至翻過越道峠從小川回到黑部平原地區。另一站則是鐘釣站，附近有美山和鐘釣兩處溫泉，也有山莊可以住宿，非常方便，只可惜這次我們目標並不在鐵道觀光，沒辦法一一下車走訪，細細品味。

至於沿途景色，說實話和太魯閣峽谷還真像，不過太魯閣所在之立霧溪比起黑部川來得更短也更陡，從下游往中游走，地形變化委實極快。黑部川的變化就顯得緩和許多，從展望開闊的礫石河灘到兩側被垂直峭壁合圍，前後綿延十幾公里，正適合以列車徐行之速度來觀察。


列車開了一個多小時，說久也並不久，但在巨大噪音和不時劇烈晃動的雙重攻擊下，來到終站欅平時雖然捨不得結束，但還真有鬆一口氣的解脫感。與「山窮水盡疑無路」的沿線景觀相比，鐵道工人繁忙的欅平無疑「柳暗花明又一村」。一整車遊客走上月台，小小的站房頓時人聲嘈雜，不過人潮來得快散得也快，沒過多久，偌大的站前廣場又恢復了寧靜。

我向車站裡的小販買了可樂餅和冰咖啡當早餐，當作是出發前的入山儀式；郭大哥和 Berny 則悠閒趴在廣場旁的欄杆上等待回程列車經過。Ariel 跟我說他幾年前曾經來過這個地方，是某一回爬山結束後的觀光行程，還在祖母谷溫泉過一夜，聽得我挺羨慕。這次出國爬山，除了我自己安排下山後花幾天繞行能登半島，其他人都隔天就回國，沒有一起旅行的時間，著實有些可惜。雖然一個人旅行自由自在，可以隨時想做什麼就做什麼，但若有旅伴共享沿途風景和際遇，為彼此生命寫下無可取代的一部分，對我來說更是非常寶貴又不能割捨的。


我想，所謂自由與不自由，或許並非單純取決於有沒有受到拘束，而是心之所向是否得到滿足吧！

Delving into the Kurobe Gorge


On a typical weekday morning, Unazuki was unusually quiet. Most shops hadn’t opened yet, and the streets were eerily empty. The atmosphere reminded me of Wulai, New Taipei, especially with the surrounding mountains. I wanted to take a leisurely stroll, but Terry urged us to buy tickets for the next leg of our journey. We still had half an hour before departure, and I thought Terry was being overly cautious. However, upon entering the station, I was surprised to find the hall already packed with people in line.

With Ariel and Berny, both fluent in Japanese, leading the way, buying tickets was a breeze. In fact, booking mountain huts and checking real-time trail conditions were also made easy thanks to them. Terry, like me, doesn’t speak Japanese, but he took on the heavy responsibility of planning the itinerary and researching information. Among the four of us, I contributed the least. In recent years, I’ve become accustomed to following along without much input.

After the pandemic began, I met Justin, who was actively inviting people to complete the 100 Peaks (a.k.a. Baiyue) of Taiwan. All I had to do was set aside time and wait for the plan, occasionally suggesting routes I wanted to explore. Later, I met Pei, Yi-hua, and Misha. They always found fun itineraries, making hiking more than just a point-to-point march to the summit. They embraced nature fully—doing yoga, meditating, drawing, or simply lying down, sitting, chatting, or being silent—making one feel part of the universe. Then I met Terry, who often proposed routes I’d never heard of, usually unmarked or secondary paths. They weren’t necessarily far, difficult, or time-consuming, but they were always filled with stories and mystery. Sometimes, we’d trek through mud or cut through dense vegetation just to see a dilapidated retaining wall or a hidden mine in a valley. Despite the different hiking styles with various people, whether fast or slow, I felt comfortable entrusting myself to them. We could share joyful moments and overcome challenges together. As a result, I became increasingly reliant and less inclined to handle the tedious preparatory work of route planning. Fortunately, everyone was accommodating, allowing me to be a “freeloader” without pressure.


As a dedicated freeloader, I hadn’t researched the origins of the Kurobe Gorge Railway before the trip. It wasn’t until long after returning home that I did some homework for this record. The railway was initially constructed after World War I by Nippon Electric Power Company to develop hydroelectric resources in the middle reaches of the Kurobe River, meeting the post-war industrial demand for electricity. After World War II, the company dissolved, and the railway changed hands several times before Kansai Electric Power Company took over. Eventually, the section from Unazuki to Keyakidaira, known as the “Lower Railway” and operational before World War II, was developed into a passenger tourist route. In 2013, it became a sister railway to the Alishan Forest Railway of Taiwan, even offering reciprocal ticketing promotions.

The Kurobe Gorge Railway offers different seating options. We chose the open-air cars at the front of the train. While they have canopies for shade, the cars only have benches and railings, with safety chains more symbolic than functional—much like amusement park rides. Since it was summer with cool, dry weather, riding in the open-air cars was enjoyable. However, in winter or rain, it might be less pleasant. The rear cars are enclosed, protecting passengers from the elements, but viewing the scenery through windows might lessen the experience.


With a sharp whistle piercing the quiet spa town, the locomotive roared to life, emitting a deep rumble. The train swayed as it slowly navigated multiple switches, passing through rough, narrow, dark tunnels. Crossing the crimson Shin-Yamabiko Bridge over the Kurobe River, people along the shore paused their work to wave enthusiastically at the train. With mixed feelings of anticipation and reluctance, we bid farewell to Unazuki Onsen, embarking on our journey into the Kurobe Gorge.

Although it’s a tourist railway, traces of its industrial past are evident everywhere. The Kurobe Gorge Railway never ceased its industrial transport role; it’s still vital for Kansai Electric Power Company to maintain the dams and power plants along the Kurobe River. After crossing the Shin-Yamabiko Bridge, we soon reached Yanagibashi Station above the Unazuki Dam. This isn’t a tourist stop but a hub for personnel and materials for the Shin-Yanagihara Power Plant, so the train slowed down but didn’t stop. Before reaching Keyakidaira, we passed the Dashidaira Dam, Kurobe River No. 2 Power Plant, and Koyadaira Dam. These places have no other access routes, highlighting the railway’s crucial role. There are two stations where tourists can disembark: Kuro-nagi Station at the confluence of the Kuro-nagi River, where visitors can hike to Kuro-nagi Onsen, Kuro-nagi No. 2 Power Plant, and Kita-mata Valley, or cross the Koedo Pass to return to the Kurobe Plain. The other is Kanetsuri Station, near Miyama and Kanetsuri Onsens, with mountain lodges available. Unfortunately, our goal wasn’t railway tourism this time, so we couldn’t explore each stop in depth.

As for the scenery along the way, it closely resembles Taiwan’s Taroko Gorge. However, the Liwu River in Taroko is shorter and steeper than the Kurobe River, with rapid terrain changes from downstream to midstream. The Kurobe River’s changes are more gradual, transitioning from open gravel beaches to vertical cliffs over tens of kilometers, making it ideal for leisurely observation from the train.


The train ride lasted just over an hour—not too long, but after enduring the deafening noise and occasional violent shaking, there was an undeniable sense of relief when we finally arrived at the terminus, Keyakidaira. Although we felt reluctant for the ride to end, the sensation of stepping off the train was liberating. Compared to the “seemingly impassable” landscapes we passed along the way, the bustling activity of railway workers at Keyakidaira felt like a surprising oasis—a “hidden village beyond the bamboo grove.” As the train emptied, the small station building quickly filled with noise and chatter, but the crowd dissipated just as fast. Before long, the spacious square in front of the station returned to its peaceful state.

I bought a croquette and iced coffee from a vendor at the station, treating it as a ceremonial breakfast before heading into the mountains. Terry and Berny, however, leaned leisurely on the railing by the square, watching the return trains pass by. Ariel mentioned that she had visited this area a few years ago after a hiking trip. She even stayed overnight at the nearby Baba-dani onsen, which left me feeling a bit envious. For this trip, I had planned to spend a few days exploring the Noto Peninsula after descending the mountain, but everyone else was heading back home the next day. It was unfortunate that we didn’t have more time to travel together. While traveling alone offers freedom to do whatever you want at any moment, having companions to share the scenery and encounters along the way creates memories that become irreplaceable parts of each other’s lives. For me, such shared experiences are precious and impossible to let go of.


I think the idea of freedom and constraint isn’t solely about whether one is bound by rules or obligations, but whether the desires of one’s heart are fulfilled.
